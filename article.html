How Many New Backlinks Do Top-Ranking Pages Get Over Time?

TS
Tim Soulo
CMO, Ahrefs
September 18, 2018
10 min read

Do top-ranking pages get new links at a faster pace than lower-ranking pages? Ask almost any experienced SEO, and they'll say "yes." But nobody had been able to prove this claim — until now.

Take the #1 ranking page for a popular keyword in your niche, and look at its referring domains report. You'll notice a steady, rising curve of new backlinks accumulating month over month. Now take a look at the page ranking in position #8 for that same keyword. The pace of link acquisition is nowhere near the same.

So the question becomes: do top-ranking pages rank high because they get lots of links — or do they get lots of links because they rank high?

Answer: It's both. This phenomenon is what we call the "vicious circle of SEO."

What Is the 'Vicious Circle of SEO'?

The vicious circle of SEO is the theory that people are most likely to link to top-ranking pages. As a result, those pages acquire links at a faster pace than all other pages on a given topic — making them almost impossible to beat in the SERPs.

A self-reinforcing loop that makes top-ranking pages incredibly difficult to displace.

1. Top-Ranking Pages Get More Exposure

Pages that rank high in Google get exposed to large numbers of people, since most users click one of the first few results. When someone later needs to reference a resource on a topic, they naturally link to the page they read — the top-ranking page. They can't link to a lower-ranking page because they never saw it in the first place.

2. Top-Ranking Pages Have More Credibility

Most top-ranking pages carry enormous credibility in the eyes of searchers simply because of where they rank. By placing a page at the top, Google is signalling: "This is the best resource in the world for your topic." So it's no surprise that content creators are eager to reference these pages when looking for something authoritative to cite.

Does the 'Vicious Circle' Hold True at Scale?

About the Experiment

Ahrefs took 10,000 non-branded keywords with monthly search volumes in the 2–5K range and pulled the top-20 ranking pages (in the US) for each — resulting in 200,000 pages analysed. They then measured how many new followed referring domains each page acquired over the previous three months.

The results confirmed the theory at scale. The top-3 search results consistently generate more new followed referring domains than the rest of the pages in the SERP — and there is a clear positive correlation between newly-acquired backlinks and ranking position.

⚠️ Important Note

Correlation ≠ causation. While there's strong belief that pages earn backlinks naturally as a byproduct of ranking high, this experiment can neither confirm nor disprove the causal direction. To establish cause and effect, a different experimental design would be needed.

How Fast Do Top-Ranking Pages Get New Backlinks?

To understand the pace of link acquisition, Ahrefs calculated the percentage by which backlink profiles grew over three months. This accounts for the fact that a page with 500 referring domains gaining 50 new links (+10%) is very different from a page with 100 RDs gaining 50 links (+50%).

+5% to +14.5%
Monthly backlink growth rate for most #1-ranking pages (followed referring domains from new websites)

This is a powerful statistic to have when a client asks: "How many backlinks do we need to build to outrank the #1 ranking page?"

Say you're targeting a keyword where the top page has links from 55 referring domains. You might think you just need to match that number. But if it takes you three months to do so, the #1 page will have grown by +17% to +50% in that same window — meaning it'll now have links from between 64 and 83 referring domains. You'd need to build as many as 83 links in three months, just to keep up.

As the ranking position declines, so does the median pace of link growth. The spread between positions is clearest at the very top — the gap between #1 and #4 is far greater than the gap between #7 and #9.

Pro Tip

Want to know exactly how fast the #1 ranking page for your target keyword is acquiring links? Open Ahrefs' Site Explorer, plug in the URL, and check the Referring Domains graph. The slope of the curve gives you a real-world benchmark for the link velocity you need to compete.

Does Acquiring Links Faster Actually Move Your Rankings?

Here's where the data gets surprising. Ahrefs tried to study whether there was a meaningful correlation between link acquisition rate and SERP movement — comparing pages that ranked in the top 3 both then and three months earlier.

The result? No clear relationship was found.

There are two likely reasons for this:

First, not all links are created equal. Sheer quantity of referring domains matters far less than the quality and relevance of those links. A single editorial link from a domain authority of 80 can outweigh dozens of low-quality links.

Second, backlinks are only one of many ranking factors. Google also accounts for behavioural signals (like dwell time), content quality, and on-page experience — none of which are captured by link velocity metrics.

Methodology Note

For each SERP, Ahrefs calculated each page's "share" of all followed referring domains among the top-20 results. They then compared each page's share at two points in time (3 months apart) and plotted whether pages that gained share also gained ranking positions. No consistent pattern emerged.

Key Takeaways

  • Top-ranking pages do acquire backlinks faster than lower-ranking pages — at a median rate of +5% to +14.5% per month in new referring domains.
  • The top 3 positions attract noticeably more new links than positions 4–10, confirming the vicious circle theory at scale.
  • When calculating how many links you need to rank, always factor in how fast your competitor is growing — not just their current count.
  • Faster link acquisition alone does not guarantee rank improvements. Link quality, relevance, and other ranking factors play a crucial role.
  • The timeless SEO advice still holds: build quality links, and create helpful, user-friendly content that people genuinely enjoy.
